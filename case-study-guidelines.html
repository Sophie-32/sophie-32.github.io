How I built content foundations for a design system from scratch

Published 8 UX content guidelines adopted across 4 product teams, reducing ad hoc copy questions by roughly 70%.

Challenge

When I joined Canonical in 2023, there was a copy style guide managed by the comms and marketing team, and I contributed to it. But it had a significant gap. It didn't cover UX copy. Designers and engineers writing interface copy had nowhere to go. The only other reference was a YAML file covering inclusive language. Without shared UX content standards, everyone made their own decisions.

After months of observation I started to see the same problems showing up across products: passive voice, verbose language that didn't need to be, and inconsistent structures for buttons and notifications. Nobody was doing it wrong on purpose. There just wasn't anything to point to that said what right looked like.

My role

I owned this project end to end. I identified the need, did the research, ran the stakeholder sessions, presented to the whole company, got approval across multiple teams, and published the guidelines. I also incorporated the existing inclusive language YAML file so nothing was lost and the guidelines became a single source of truth.

I worked closely with the comms and marketing team throughout. They owned the copy style guide and we had a great working relationship. The goal was always for the UX content guidelines to complement their work, not compete with it.

Discovery

I spent several months observing before I wrote a single guideline. I tracked the questions I was getting, noted where inconsistencies kept appearing, and ran training sessions with at least three teams including design, content and comms, and product managers. I also presented UX writing best practices in a five minute talk at Canonical's company plenary in front of more than 1,000 people.

Those sessions were research as much as they were training. They told me what people were confused about, what they needed most, and what would actually get used versus what would sit unread.

I then benchmarked content guidelines from 6 other design systems including Material Design, Carbon, and Atlassian. I looked at what they covered, how they structured their guidance, and where they fell short for Canonical's specific context. Our contributors and audience often have English as a second language, so practical and actionable guidance mattered more than abstract principles.

Before and after
Before
  • One YAML file for inclusive language, no UX content standards
  • Designers and engineers making independent decisions on copy
  • Passive voice, verbose language, inconsistent button and notification copy
  • 5 to 6 ad hoc content questions per month outside of normal design reviews
After
  • 8 UX content guidelines live in the design system
  • Single source of truth incorporating the existing inclusive language standards
  • Adopted across 4 product teams
  • Ad hoc questions down to 1 to 2 per month
Rationale

I grounded every guideline in three principles: clarity, consistency, and inclusivity. I gave concrete examples and decision trees rather than vague advice. Character limits with actual numbers rather than "keep it short." Real button label examples rather than abstract rules. Guidance that works for non-native English speakers, not just fluent ones.

I also built the guidelines around what I was already teaching in training sessions so the documentation reinforced messages teams had already heard rather than introducing something new.
